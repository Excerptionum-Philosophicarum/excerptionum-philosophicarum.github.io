Dianoia Collection

The Dianoia Collection is comprised of haikus on various science-fictional themes (as well as about the genre), often referencing particular writers, stories, or worldbuilding contexts. In a strict sense, however, especially in the thematic aspect, these poems are not traditional Japanese haiku, nor are they precisely senryū, either. Nonetheless, I like adhering to the typical structure, concerning (1) syllabic metrics, and (2) seeking to pivot, as inspired by the disjuncture produced in Japanese via the kireji (切れ字, ‘cutting word’). Very importantly, in the spirit of this kind of poetry, I enjoy practicing its density in brevity, together with an exploration of intuition, connected with the meditative aspect it tends to have. For pragmatism, therefore, whenever I use the term “haiku” I mean it sensu lato, mostly in reference to my own writings. For greater accuracy, I can suggest expressions like “haikuesque” or “haiku-like” instead of “haiku”, but the latter is usually the better-known in the Occidental world.

I

Ia

Speculative hill
where I study strange flowers
aroma of stars.

Ib

In synthetic tales
I analyse swervingly
analog planets.

Ic

By means of fiction
re-approach reality
distance affection.

II

IIa

Stellar radiance
which we sense from our capsules
we focus mirrors.

IIb

Archaeologists
interpret the ancient gate
a future threshold.

IIc

Beautiful cluster
but with blood-tinged nebulae
the free roamers fly.

IId

A simulacrum
anarcho-capitalist?
Infomorphs ponder.

III

IIIa

Searching urbanite
experiences wanderthought
mystified frontiers.

IIIb

Post-scarcity son
beneath a conditioned dome
horizon instinct.

IV

IVa

The galactic state
pictures a classical past
potential present.

IVb

Mathematicians
err psychohistorically
non-linear ways.

V

We alternate views
to see juxtaposed cultures
world sign variance.

VI

VIa

They nourish neurons
with subcellular machines
cognitive mists swirl.

VIb

They sustain axons
linking throngs of iotas
kinesis endures.

VII

Cyborg nutrition
from molecule assemblers
a spoon feeds itself.

VIII

Scientists approach
mercury cnidarians
in Yatsushiro.
